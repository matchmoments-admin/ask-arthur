# 136 fake Aussie retail stores appeared in one month. We watched them do it.

In June 2026, Ask Arthur's clone-watch system — the same monitoring that scans newly registered domains every day for lookalikes of about 130 Australian brands — logged **804 lookalike domains**. The single biggest cluster wasn't banks, and it wasn't crypto.

It was **retail**.

- **Target Australia:** 43 lookalike domains in June
- **Kmart:** 28
- **Bonds:** 25
- **Coles:** 23
- **Amazon Australia:** 17

That's **136 fake versions of five of Australia's most-trusted shops, registered in a single month** — almost entirely during the EOFY sales, when every Australian inbox and feed is full of genuine discount ads and one more "70% off, today only" store doesn't look out of place.

That timing is not a coincidence. It's the business model.

---

## What these fake stores actually are

A retail clone is a website built to pass as a brand's real store: the logo, the product photos, the fonts, sometimes entire pages copied wholesale. It lives on a domain that's _almost_ right, and it exists to do one of three things:

- **Take payment for goods that never arrive.** The most common version. The "store" runs checkout, charges your card, and ships nothing.
- **Harvest your card details.** Some clones don't even bother pretending to ship — the checkout form is the product.
- **Collect your personal details** — name, address, phone, email — for follow-up scams that arrive later and know exactly who you are.

These are real domains our system watched go from "just registered" to serving a live storefront in the past few weeks:

- `targett.shop` — one extra letter (went live 10 June)
- `bombondshq.shop` — Bonds, roughly (21 June)
- `kkmartdirect.shop` and `kkmartcentral.shop` — a matching pair of fake Kmarts, one day apart (23–24 June)
- `taget.one` — one _missing_ letter (3 July)
- `targetshopp.cc` (5 July)
- `amazoniasshop.shop` (7 July)

Don't visit them. The point of listing them is the pattern: **a real brand name, slightly wrong, on a domain ending the real brand would never use** — `.shop`, `.one`, `.cc`, `.top`.

> [!DANGER]
> None of these sites look "dodgy". Modern clone kits copy the real store's design pixel-for-pixel, and AI site builders have made a convincing fake a one-hour job. You cannot reliably spot a fake store by how it looks. You spot it by its **address**.

---

## Why June? Why retail?

Three forces stack up.

**Sale season is camouflage.** EOFY runs the whole month. Genuine retailers really are discounting hard, really are buying social ads, really are sending urgent emails. A fake "Kmart clearance — up to 80% off" ad slides straight into that noise. The same spike will repeat for Black Friday in November and Boxing Day.

**Domains are nearly free.** The endings you see over and over in our data — `.shop`, `.top`, `.cc`, `.online` — can cost a couple of dollars, registered in bulk, in minutes, anonymously. When one fake Kmart gets taken down, the operator has three more waiting. That's why we logged 28 Kmart lookalikes in one month, not one.

**The ads find you.** You don't stumble onto `bombondshq.shop` by typing it. It reaches you as a social media ad, a marketplace listing, or a text. Scamwatch's reporting consistently shows **online shopping scams among the most-reported scam types in Australia**, with social media one of the fastest-growing contact channels — and losses to them rising year on year (see scamwatch.gov.au for the current figures).

## What we do about it (and what "watched" means)

Honesty about our own numbers, because a scam-detection company should show its working:

- **804 lookalike domains detected** across ~130 monitored brands in June. Detection means the domain lexically matches a brand we watch — not that every one has a live scam on it yet. Many sit parked, waiting.
- Our system **rechecks the suspicious ones** and flags the moment a parked domain "weaponises" — starts serving real content. Thirty-seven did that in the last two months; every example named above was caught this way.
- **628 of June's detections were reported onward** to takedown services and blocklist providers. Some get actioned in days; some get declined until the site turns visibly malicious — which is exactly why we keep watching after we report.

You can see the live numbers any time on our public [clone-watch page](https://askarthur.au/clone-watch).

## The ten-second address check

Everything above condenses to this. Before you pay any store you reached from an ad, a text, or a marketplace link:

**1. Read the domain right-to-left.** The only part that matters is the _end_: `kmart.com.au` is Kmart. `kkmartdirect.shop` is not, no matter what the logo says.

**2. Distrust the ending.** Australian retailers overwhelmingly live on `.com.au`. A big-brand "store" on `.shop`, `.top`, `.cc`, `.one` or `.online` is a red flag on its own.

**3. Count the letters.** `taget`, `targett`, `kkmart`, `bombonds` — doubling or dropping one letter is the oldest trick in the book because it still works.

**4. Don't trust the ad; open a new tab.** If Kmart is really running that sale, it's on kmart.com.au. Type it yourself.

**5. Too-deep discounts on everything.** Real EOFY sales discount _some_ lines. Fake stores discount the entire catalogue 70–90%, permanently.

## If you already paid one

Move fast — speed matters more than embarrassment. Nobody who got taken by a pixel-perfect fake Target during a sale rush was being stupid.

1. **Contact your bank immediately** and ask for a chargeback on the card payment. If you paid by card, you have real recourse; if the site asked for a bank transfer or PayID, call your bank anyway — the [PayID version of this scam](https://askarthur.au/blog/payid-scams-facebook-marketplace-guide) has its own playbook.
2. **Report it to Scamwatch** at scamwatch.gov.au — reports drive the official takedown and warning machinery.
3. **If your card details went in, cancel the card**, not just the transaction.
4. **Watch for the follow-up.** Your details are now on a list. The "refund team" that calls next week _is the same scam, round two_. Our guide on [what to do if you've been scammed](https://askarthur.au/blog/what-to-do-if-youve-been-scammed-australia) covers the full sequence.

The fake stores will keep coming — 804 in June says the economics work. But every one of them has to put its real address in your browser bar, and that's the one thing they can't fake. Ten seconds on the domain beats any logo.
