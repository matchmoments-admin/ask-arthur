### Anticipated Objections

**Objection 1: "Solo founder risk — what if you get hit by a bus?"**

Direct answer: valid concern. Three mitigations. First, the codebase is well-documented (architecture docs, OpenAPI spec, monorepo with clear package boundaries) — a competent engineer can pick it up [Data]. Second, first hire is Head of Sales, not another engineer — the technical foundation is solid, the gap is distribution [Assumption]. Third, actively seeking co-founder through Startmate or CyRise cohort, and building advisory board with cybersecurity and financial services expertise [Data — plan, not yet executed]. The solo-founder phase is the most capital-efficient way to build a production-ready platform. The next phase requires a team.

**Objection 2: "You're pre-revenue — where's the evidence anyone will pay?"**

The SPF Act creates regulatory demand that doesn't depend on us convincing anyone scams are a problem. 200+ entities face $52.7M penalties starting 1 July 2026 [Data]. The question is not "will they buy scam intelligence?" — it's "from whom?" Our path to first revenue is a POC pilot with a mid-tier bank or credit union, converting to paid within 6 months. We're not asking investors to bet on a market that might exist. The market is being legislated into existence [Data for regulation, Assumption for conversion].

**Objection 3: "What stops a larger player (CrowdStrike, Palo Alto) from entering?"**

Two things. First, global security vendors optimise for enterprise-only, top-down sales. They don't build free consumer tools that generate community-sourced data — that's a fundamentally different GTM motion [Assumption]. Second, the data flywheel compounds. A competitor starting today would need 24+ months of Australian community-sourced scam data to match our threat database [Assumption]. Enterprise vendors are also unlikely to build Australian-specific products for a $200-500M SAM when their existing markets are much larger [Assumption].

**Objection 4: "The Big Four banks are building in-house — doesn't that shrink your market?"**

Yes, and that's the plan. The Big Four (CBA, NAB, ANZ, Westpac) have the budgets to build proprietary solutions. We don't compete with them — we serve the 80+ mid-tier banks, credit unions, telcos, and digital platforms that can't build in-house [Estimate for count]. Longer term, even Big Four banks benefit from cross-sector intelligence they can't generate alone. Our positioning is complementary, not competitive [Assumption].

**Objection 5: "A$5M-$8M cap seems high for pre-revenue."**

The product is built. 11 development phases, 44 database migrations, 7 consumer surfaces, 6 API endpoints — this represents 12+ months of full-time engineering work that would cost $300K-$500K to replicate [Estimate]. The cap reflects the technical asset, the regulatory tailwind, and the narrow timing window. Comparable Australian cybersecurity pre-seeds (Apollo Secure at $600K, SecurePII at Tidal's range) set similar benchmarks [Data].
